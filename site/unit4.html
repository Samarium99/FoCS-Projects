    Precipitation Reactions

    (a type of double replacement reaction) where two molecules react, pairing up to form a solid precipate.

    Example:

    Cl+ and NO3 dissociate, and would not react with other components, however Pb2+ is an exception for Cl, combining to form a PbCl2 precipate. NO3 does not have any exceptions, so it and H+ do not react. $$2HCl_{(aq)} + Pb(NO_{3})_{2(aq)} \rightarrow 2HNO_{3(aq)} + PbCl_{2(s)}$$

    Net Ionic Equations

    when writing the equation, cross out molecules that do not change. Since some constituents are spectator ions, they will dissociate and will not change. However, in NAGSAG exceptions, when the molecule does not dissociate, it changes and should be recorded in the net ionic equation.

    $$2H^{+} + 2Cl^{-} + Pb^{2+} + 2NO_{3}^{-} \rightarrow 2H^{+}+ 2NO_{3}^{-} + PbCl_{2}$$

    $$Pb^{2+} + 2Cl^{-} \rightarrow PbCl_{2(s)}$$

    Spectator Ion Exceptions
    N Nitrate $$NO_{3} ^{-}$$ n/a
    A Acetate $$C_{2}H_{3}O_{2}$$ n/a
    G Group 1 $$Li^{+}, Na^{+}, K^{+}, etc.$$ n/a
    S Sulfate $$SO_{4-}$$ $$Ca^{2+}, Sr^{2+}, Ba^{2+}, Pb^{2+}, Hg^{2+}, Ag^{2+}$$
    A Ammonium $$NH_{4}^{+}$$ n/a
    G Group 7 $$F^{-}, Cl^{-}, Br^{-}, etc.$$ Pb^{2+}, Hg^{2+}, Ag^{2+}
    Compounds are soluble in water if they contain a spectator ion. Spectator ions dissociate when aqueous and do not react in water (unless exceptions)

    Acids and Bases

    Acids, according to Bronsted definitions, are compounds which are H+ donors. Bases are H+ acceptors. Strong Acids and Bases fully dissociate in water. Weak Acids/Bases partially dissociate in water (equilibrium unit).

    Strong Acid + Strong Base --> H2O

    Strong Acid + Weak Base (or vice versa) --> conjugate Acid + conjugate Base

    7 main Strong Acids:

    • HCl
    • HBr
    • HI
    • HNO3
    • H2SO4
    • HClO4
    • HClO3

    Strong Bases:

    Cation is from group 1 or 2 + OH-
